It used to be that choosing a color for your deck was a biennial affair, a parade of going from transparent stains to semi-transparents and finally paint as you attempted to extend the life of your treated lumber outdoor space.<\/p>\n

With the advent of low-maintenance composite decking, choosing the best deck color for your home is less about drudgery than about design. Making the right choice is crucial and encompasses more than simply knowing the most popular deck colors.<\/p>\n

What goes into picking a deck color? Here are some things to consider:<\/p>\n

Consult a Color Wheel<\/h2>\n

A typical color scheme in design consists of three colors: dominant, secondary and accent. Unless you\u2019re matching the home\u2019s exterior color exactly (here\u2019s a tip: don\u2019t try), the deck should be the secondary color. If not matching the home\u2019s trim or extending the look of adjacent interior flooring to the outside, consult a color wheel.<\/p>\n

From a color wheel\u2019s 12 pie pieces, you can go directly opposite the dominant color (yellow to violet, for example) to find complementary colors. Analogous colors, or those on either side of the dominant (in this case, yellow and green or yellow and orange), also work.<\/p>\n

Does Deck Color Impact Home Value?<\/h2>\n

The right deck color can absolutely enhance your home\u2019s value, just as the wrong one can be a negative. Of course, when it comes time to sell your home, the most important aspect of your deck is that it is well-maintained \u2014 clean, uncluttered and structurally stable. But first impressions being what they are, a deck color that is cohesive with the home\u2019s palette and appropriate to the style of the structure may decide whether a prospective buyer even crosses the threshold.<\/p>\n

Colors to Consider<\/h2>\n

You might have regional reasons to favor one color over another \u2014 warm climates suggest lighter colors for comfort, and lighter colors don\u2019t show daily dustings of pollen in the spring. You may have situational reasons \u2014 the mud tracked by kids and dogs won\u2019t show as readily on dark decks. Regardless, here are some guidelines:<\/p>\n

Dark tones<\/strong>, such as NewTechWood\u2019s Hawaiian Charcoal, add a splash of drama to any space. A little can go a long way, however, and darker shades hold more heat from direct sun, so consider lighter colors for larger projects, borders and stairs. The payoff will be a very contemporary look.<\/p>\n

For more of a beachy, sun-washed vibe, light grays<\/strong> (Westminster Gray or Icelandic Smoke White) are very effective. Reds<\/strong> like NewTechWood\u2019s Swedish Red might seem daring, but they actually play as rather traditional (think barn red) \u2014 especially when set off against white trim, railings and stair risers.<\/p>\n

Wood tones<\/strong> (Spanish Walnut, Brazilian Ipe, Peruvian Teak) enhance the nature surrounding the deck and are generally safer bets when resale is a consideration. Make sure to take note of undertones within the grain. For example, some browns can read a bit reddish, while others lean toward yellow.<\/p>\n

Consult With an Expert<\/h2>\n

Remember, a knowledgeable pro can help guide you. If you haven\u2019t contemplated the full variety of patterns, board direction, border colors, etc., available to you, talk to an expert.<\/p>\n
