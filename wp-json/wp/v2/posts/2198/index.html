Installing Deck Tiles on Grass<\/h2>\n

If you have decided to install a new ground-level composite deck in your yard, you may be wondering if you can put deck tiles on grass or if it is safe to put deck tiles on grass. If so, NewTechWood\u00ae is here to help. In this guide, we\u2019ll discuss the process of installing deck tiles on grass \u2014 including why you should never do it and what you can do instead.<\/p>\n<\/div>\n

<\/div>\n
\n

Can You Put Deck Tiles on Grass?<\/h2>\n

You should never install\u00a0NewTechWood capped composite deck tiles<\/a>\u00a0directly on grass or dirt\u00a0\u2014 the main reasons for this include:<\/p>\n<\/div>\n

<\/div>\n
<\/div>\n
\n
    \n
  • Moisture damage:\u00a0<\/strong>Even in yards with excellent drainage systems in place, water and moisture can collect in your soil, particularly in snowy or rainy conditions. Ground moisture from direct contact with grass can damage your composite deck tiles over time.<\/li>\n
  • Mold growth:<\/strong>\u00a0When you lay decking tiles directly on the dirt, you eliminate airflow. Lack of proper airflow can lead to mold or bacterial growth on your composite tiles.<\/li>\n
  • Unstable ground:<\/strong>\u00a0Think about the last time you walked across your yard \u2014 did the grass move under your shoes? Was the dirt soft and flexible? Shifting, unstable ground can move your deck over time, which can lead to costly repairs or replacements.<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n

     <\/p>\n

    Can You Lay Composite Decking on Grass?<\/h2>\n

    Just as it is with deck tiles, you cannot lay composite decking directly on grass. You will face the same issues, including potential for mold growth and ground movement. Instead, place your\u00a0NewTechWood UltraShield\u00ae Naturale\u2122<\/a>\u00a0on solid surfaces, such as:<\/p>\n

      \n
    • Concrete sidewalks or other paved areas<\/li>\n
    • A pre-existing patio<\/li>\n
    • On top of compact sand or ground-up gravel<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n

       <\/p>\n

      How to Create an Acceptable Area for Deck Tiles?<\/h2>\n

      Should you put deck tiles on grass? The short answer is no. However, just because you shouldn\u2019t put deck tiles on grass, doesn\u2019t mean you\u2019re without options. If you want to know how to lay decking tiles on grass, the solution is to first properly prepare the section of your yard you wish to lay your composite decking on. To create an acceptable area for deck tiles, follow these tips:<\/p>\n

        \n
      • Avoid excessively wet areas:\u00a0<\/strong>As you choose the part of your yard on which you want to install composite decking, make sure you avoid overly wet areas, including deep depressions or places where standing water frequently gathers.<\/li>\n
      • Watch out for tree roots:<\/strong>\u00a0Although the exact distance varies depending on the type of tree, most established root systems extend several feet beyond the tree trunk. When placing your new deck, keep this in mind. Avoid laying your deck over a known root system, as this can damage your tree and may cause problems with your deck in the future.<\/li>\n
      • Level the ground:\u00a0<\/strong>Before you place your composite decking, you need to level the ground. If the area is small enough, you can do this by hand using a shovel, hoe and rake. For larger surface areas, you may need to use a tiller or heavy equipment.<\/li>\n
      • Establish a firm base:<\/strong>\u00a0Grass and dirt are unstable and can shift over time. To prevent this, you need to create a firm base on top of your newly leveled ground. Construct your base out of poured concrete, tightly compacted sand or grounded gravel. This base will act as a supportive, protective layer between your tiles and the ground beneath.<\/li>\n
      • Ensure adequate airflow:\u00a0<\/strong>Proper airflow beneath your composite deck tiles is crucial.\u00a0If you are using NewTechWood composite decking, we recommend you leave a 250 to 300-millimeter clearance between your decking and the grass or dirt. For concrete or pavement, a 40 to 50-millimeter clearance is adequate.<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n
